Math 101, College Algebra, is a 3-credit course designed to deepen students’ understanding of functions and prepare them for success in subsequent mathematics courses.  In this course you will study many types of functions including linear, quadratic, polynomial, rational, exponential, logarithmic, and piecewise, and learn to use functions to model real-world situations. 

Students with previous credit in any calculus course (that is, Math 104 or Math 106, 107, (or 208 or their honors versions) may not register for or earn credit toward their degree in this course or any other math course numbered 104 or below.  Written permission from the math department advisor to take this course will be granted for reasonable requests (for example, to take Math 102 after Math 104 to satisfy the prerequisites of Math 106).  If you have questions or need permission, please contact your academic advisor or the math department advisor. 

Course Prerequisites: Appropriate score on the Math Placement Exam; or grade of P, C, or better in Math 100A. The Department of Mathematics will drop all students who do not satisfy this policy (review the Math Placement Policy at http://www.math.unl.edu/resources/undergraduate/mpe/).
Textbook: Mathematics: PreCalculus Mathematics at Nebraska by Funk, Setniker, Uhing, Wakefield. This is a free digital textbook!

Your Responsibilities as a Class Member: 
Regular, on-time attendance and active participation in class activities until class is dismissed. 
Be ready to concentrate on math and discuss the day’s material. Once class starts, it is not a place for casual conversations, reading the newspaper, doing homework for other classes, etc. 
Stay on task.  No texting, reading messages or browsing the web with a phone, tablet or computer. 
Be respectful and polite.  Listen to your instructor and your fellow students when they are talking. 
If you understand the mathematics, help others learn.  Mathematics is not a competitive game and we want everyone to succeed.  Explaining your understanding to others not only helps others learn but it is a great way to strengthen your own understanding of the mathematics. 
In order to benefit from being in an interactive class, each student must come to class prepared. 
Come to class having done the assigned reading and watched the assigned videos. 
 

Why We Emphasize Writing: Students sometimes ask, “Why do we have to do all this writing?  Writing has nothing to do with mathematics!”  Our belief is that writing is a crucial part of the thinking process itself.  Writing explanations of your work will improve your understanding.  The more carefully and clearly you write your mathematics, the more likely it is to be correct, and the more likely you will be to remember it. 

The answer to a math problem is not a number, but an explanation of how that number was obtained. 

 

Why We Encourage a Team Approach to Learning and Doing Mathematics: By working in a team, you will learn the material more thoroughly and remember it better.  Out in the “real world”, you will spend most of your working life as part of a team and you can start working on being an effective team member now.  The ability to communicate (and understand) technical material is particularly important. 

 

Working Productively to Learn and Help Others Learn as Members of a Team: The ability to work productively as part of a team and to ensure that everyone learns with and from the other members of the group is an essential part of this course.  When work is completed and assignments submitted, every member of the group should be able to explain how to solve each problem.  Here are some suggestions from students at Michigan who were asked what it takes for a team to function at its full potential. 

Schedule enough meetings, and don’t schedule them at the last minute. 
Go to every meeting and be on time.  (Woody Allen says, 80% of life is just showing up.) 
Do the reading and work on each of the problems before the group meets 
Find a way to express varying opinions in a friendly way. 
Listen carefully.  Don’t interrupt and don’t tune out. 
Make sure that everyone is equally involved. 
Avoid making others feel dumb. 
Stay on the topic. 
Don’t rush to finish before everyone understands.

Course Structure and Expectations: 
This course will likely be quite different from previous math courses you have taken. Rather than overloading you with numerous exercises of the exact same type, this course will focus on enhancing your understanding of algebraic skills through novel problems that require a heightened level of critical thinking and reasoning. In other words, rote memorization is likely not a skill that will help you in this course. 

Lecture Format: A typical class will include a mixture of lecture by the instructor and group work and will cover approximately one section per day corresponding to the course’s online textbook. Lecture will be time for the instructor to go over any important material and concepts for the day’s lesson. The group work portion of the class will often resemble student being split into groups and to work collaboratively on problems from worksheets in your course packet. You are expected to engage in meaningful discussion and debate with your fellow group members.
Course Packet: The worksheets in the course packetLinks to an external site. are designed to help you prepare for homework assignments and assessments. You do not need to do the worksheets before class as a large portion of each class period will be spent working on these worksheets. Although this will not be graded directly, you will be expected to fill out the course packet during class time as part of your learning and active participation in the class. 
Online Homework: For each week of class, there is typically two to three corresponding online homework sets. Your instructor will inform you of the deadlines; it is your responsibility to complete them on time. Information on how to access the online homework can be found later in this syllabus.
Attendance: Since much of the learning in this course occurs interactively during class time, regular, on-time attendance is essential. If you are sick and therefore unable to attend class, you should contact your instructor before class if possible.
Time Commitment: This course requires a solid effort.  To be successful, you should be prepared to study (and work math problems) a minimum of two hours each week outside class for each credit hour.  I.e., we expect you to spend at least 6 hours per week outside of class working on mathematics.

Assessment Format and Grading: 
Course Readiness Activity (CRA): We want all students to be successful in this course, but from past experience we know that some students start the course having forgotten the mathematics background knowledge needed for success.  To ensure all students have retained and mastered this essential background knowledge and skills, we administer a Course Readiness Activity (CRA) during the first two weeks of class.  The activity assesses knowledge that a student should have learned in an Algebra II class in high school or by taking UNL’s Math 100A. Calculators are not permitted for the CRA.  The activity has 15 questions worth a total of 20 points, and the passing level is 16 out of 20 points.  If a student passes the CRA by Sunday January 18th at 11:59pm, they will receive 23/20 points as a congratulations for starting the semester well. Students that have attempted the CRA and attained a score of at least 10/20 by Sunday, January 18th at 11:59pm, will be guaranteed at least a score of 10/20. Students who meet the 16 out of 20 proficiency level by Tuesday, January 27th at 11:59pm will earn 20/20 points towards their course grade, thus getting off to a fast start in the course. Students who do not meet this standard receive 0/20 points. The CRA may be taken up to once every 24 hours until you achieve a passing score. The testing “window” begins Monday, January 12th and closes Tuesday, January 27th at 11:59pm. The CRA will be taken on WebWork and a link to the CRA can be found under the assignments tab. You will take the CRA unproctored and you are not allowed to use a calculator for the assessment.
WeBWork Homework: We use an online system called WeBWorK to assess the individual component of your homework in Math 101.  The web homework gives you instant feedback, practice solving problems, and a reward for making the effort to learn.  You are permitted 6 attempts for each question.  Each assignment will be weighted evenly.  There will be approximately two or three assignment due each week starting the second week of class. Typically, each assignment will correspond to a section in the Course Packet. These are usually due the day after the next lecture. (For example, Section 1.4 in the Course Packet will be covered in class on Friday (Jan. 23rd) and the corresponding WeBWork assignment (Homework 04) will be due on Tuesday the next week (Jan. 27th).)  Many of the problems on these assignments come directly from the course packet, so the best way to prepare for these is to complete the Course Packet. Each assignment is worth 10 points, and your lowest two scores will be dropped. We offer the following advice as to how to be successful on the web homework component of your assessment. 
Start early, when each set opens. This will give you more time to ask for help and resolve any issues that may arise.  
Do the example homework set, Web Homework 00.  It will help you familiarize yourself with the homework system before working Web Homework 1. 
Don’t submit partial answers.  You have six submissions per problem. 
Work the problems out by hand.  Perhaps print a hardcopy of an assignment and solve the problems before entering your solutions into the WeBWorK system. 
Preview your answers before submitting them. There is a preview button. This gives you a chance to see that what the computer is reading based on the syntax you typed into WeBWork before your final submission. 
Watch your case: A capital X is different from x, and Pi is different from pi. 
Watch your precision.  The system generally requires that numeric answers be correct to at least four or five significant figures.  I.e., if the answer is 3.472322, then 3.5 may be marked incorrect, but 3.4723 accepted. 
Seek help using the "email my instructor" button when you get stuck on a question.
Students may seek help solving WeBWorK problems from their instructor, the MRC, classmates, and even friends or tutors.  However, students are cautioned to be certain that completing WeBWorK assignments is a learning experience whose goal is to understand the material at a level that will enable you to work similar problems on an exam.  If you believe your answer to a question is correct, but WeBWorK marked it as incorrect, show your instructor a copy of your solution.  If the WeBWorK system does not give you appropriate credit, we certainly want to identify the problem and correct it.  If you have made a subtle mistake, either mathematical or with respect to syntax and how the software “reads” what you entered, we want to help you understand the mistake so that you can eliminate or minimize such errors. Finally, we have put together a page that might help with some common concerns which can be found here.

This course will use an assessment format called mastery-based grading. In mastery-based grading, students receive credit only when they have displayed "mastery" of a course outcome, but they receive multiple attempts to do so. The idea behind this grading system is to focus on improvement rather than the grade.   

In-Class Outcomes: There is a total of 14 outcomes to be completed in class. Students are allowed to use a non-graphing calculator on the in class outcomes. There will usually be one to two in-class outcomes each week and these will typically take place during the last 10 to 20 minutes of class time each Friday. For each in-class outcome, students will have an initial attempt, a second attempt (usually a week later), and a third attempt on the appropriate “Exam” day. See the class schedule for more information on the dates for these outcomes. For each outcome, there are four levels of performance that you can receive: 
Level 

Description 

Mastery  (10/10 points) 

The work is complete and clear, and any error is trivial and/or does not relate to the outcome that is being graded. The solution is easy to follow and justification is provided when necessary. 

Sufficient Mastery    (7/10 points) 

The solution displays almost complete understanding. The work is almost correct, but the justification is unclear or incomplete. 

Partially Mastered  (5/10 points) 

Partial understanding is evident, but there are significant gaps in the solution. Such gaps might relate to errors that are directly related to the outcome, communication of reasoning, or an inability to complete the problem. 

Little or No Mastery (0/10 points) 

Understanding could not be measured from the solution. Not enough work was submitted, significant errors were made, and/or the method used was inappropriate for the problem. 

Examples of what the grading may look like for In-Class Outcomes can be found in the course files in the folder entitled "Examples Graded In-Class Outcomes".
